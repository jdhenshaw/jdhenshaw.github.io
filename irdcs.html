Star formation in a highly filamentary IRDC

The physical and dynamical properties of G035.39-00.33

An important step in developing a holistic understanding of the star formation process is identifying and categorising the initial phases of high-mass star formation (stars about 10x the mass of the sun). Ultimately this requires detailed knowledge of their host molecular clouds. Identifying massive and relatively quiescent molecular clouds is therefore crucial. Following their discovery as silhouettes against the bright Galactic mid-infrared background, infrared dark clouds (IRDCs) have recently received significant attention in the field of high-mass star formation.

Initial studies set about categorizing their physical properties, finding broad ranges in size (1-10 pc), mass (102-105 solar masses), and column density (1022-1025 cm-2). Subsequent investigations categorising their temperatures (~20 K), chemistry, and kinematics have ensued. My research has focused on G035.39-00.33 (G035): a massive (16000 solar masses) and filamentary IRDC, displaying extended regions with no obvious tracers of star formation activity. Early studies identified faint, but widespread, SiO emission. As a possible "fossil record" of the cloud formation process, this archeological chemical signature suggests that large-scale dynamics may have an important role to play in regulating both cloud and star formation throughout G035.

Figure 1: A position-position-velocity diagram highlighting the kinematic structure of the IRDC G035.39-00.33. Each data point refers to the location and velocity of an individual spectral feature.

The representation of G035 as a simple singular filamentary structure, as it appears in dust continuum images, is deceptive. Instead, my research has shown that G035 can be kinematically disentangled into a network of morphologically distinct sub-filaments, each of which displays its own physical and dynamic structure (see Fig. 1). These sub-filaments spatially overlap at the location of one of the most massive (~100 solar masses) clumps within the cloud: a protocluster “hub-filament” system and potential site of high-mass star formation. We used high-angular resolution interferometric observations to reveal the presence of further sub-structure not evident in the single-dish observations. Investigating the distribution of continuum emission throughout G035, we identified a number of quasi-regularly spaced gravitationally-bound cores. Comparing the spatial distribution of the cores with that predicted by theoretical work describing the fragmentation of hydrodynamic fluid cylinders, I found a significant (factor of ~ 8) discrepancy between the two. Although our angular resolution (~0.1 pc) was still insufficient to spatially disentangle G035’s substructure, this discrepancy led me to suggest that the reality is more complex than the assumed model (a simple cylindrical singular filament). I recently confirmed this hypothesis using high-angular resolution (~0.01 pc) ALMA observations. Here, I unveiled the presence of extremely narrow (~0.03 pc) spatially-resolved filaments, embedded within the aforementioned protocluster hub (Fig. 2).

Figure 2: Left: the ALMA 1mm continuum map of the H6 hub-filament system (logarithmic colour scale in units of Jy/beam and thin black contours). Right: The median column density profile perpendicular to the filament marked `F' in the left-hand panel (thick black line). The yellow area indicates the median absolute deviation. Different models used to derive the filament width are shown in red and green. The derived filament width is of the order ~0.03 pc.

Why is this important for understanding star formation? Filaments are a key ingredient of the molecular interstellar medium. From a dynamical perspective, both observations and simulations agree that filaments play an important role in regulating star formation. In this context, filaments act as tributaries, funnelling mass towards protocluster clumps. Comparing to the popular “bead-on-a-string” analogy, however, which describes massive star-forming hubs as monolithic clumps fed over parsec scales by large-scale filaments, my research has shown that this is a poor reflection of the internal structure of G035. Instead, the protocluster clump (Fig. 2) in G035 comprises multiple intra-hub filaments, each of which retains its integrity as an independent structure and possesses its own embedded core population. The presence of small-scale (sub-pc) systematic velocity gradients along sub-filaments, in the vicinity of dust continuum peaks, suggests that the embedded core population accretes locally. This study has therefore directly challenged our understanding of how star formation is regulated by filaments in this star-forming region.
