Star formation in a highly filamentary IRDC

The physical and dynamical properties of G035.39-00.33

An important step in developing a holistic understanding of the star formation process is identifying and categorising the initial phases of high-mass star formation (stars about 10x the mass of the sun). Ultimately this requires detailed knowledge of their host molecular clouds. Identifying massive and relatively quiescent molecular clouds is therefore crucial. Following their discovery as silhouettes against the bright Galactic mid-infrared background, infrared dark clouds (IRDCs) have recently received significant attention in the field of high-mass star formation.

Initial studies set about categorizing their physical properties, finding broad ranges in size (1-10 pc), mass (102-105 solar masses), and column density (1022-1025 cm-2). Subsequent investigations categorising their temperatures (~20 K), chemistry, and kinematics have ensued. My research has focused on G035.39-00.33 (G035): a massive (16000 solar masses) and filamentary IRDC, displaying extended regions with no obvious tracers of star formation activity. Early studies identified faint, but widespread, SiO emission. As a possible "fossil record" of the cloud formation process, this archeological chemical signature suggests that large-scale dynamics may have an important role to play in regulating both cloud and star formation throughout G035.

Figure 1: A position-position-velocity diagram highlighting the kinematic structure of the IRDC G035.39-00.33. Each data point refers to the location and velocity of an individual spectral feature.

The representation of G035 as a simple singular filamentary structure, as it appears in dust continuum images, is deceptive. Instead, I have shown that G035 can be kinematically disentangled into a network of morphologically distinct sub-filaments, each of which displays its own physical and dynamic structure (see Fig. 1). These sub-filaments spatially overlap at the location of one of the most massive (~100 solar masses) clumps within the cloud: a protocluster “hub-filament” system and potential site of high-mass SF. I used high-angular resolution interferometric observations to reveal the presence of further sub-structure not evident in the single-dish observations. Investigating the distribution of continuum emission throughout G035, I identified a number of quasi-regularly spaced gravitationally-bound cores. Comparing the spatial distribution of the cores with that predicted by theoretical work describing the fragmentation of hydrodynamic fluid cylinders, I found a significant (factor of ~ 8) discrepancy between the two. Although our angular resolution (~0.1 pc) was still insufficient to spatially disentangle G035’s substructure, this discrepancy led me to suggest that the reality is more complex than the assumed model (a simple cylindrical singular filament). I recently confirmed this hypothesis using high-angular resolution (~0.01 pc) ALMA observations. Here, I unveiled the presence of extremely narrow (~0.03 pc) spatially-resolved filaments, embedded within the aforementioned protocluster hub.
