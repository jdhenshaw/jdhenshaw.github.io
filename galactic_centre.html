Star formation in the Galactic Centre

Molecular gas kinematics within the inner 250 pc of the Milky Way

The ultimate goal of star formation research is to develop an end-to-end understanding of stellar mass assembly as a function of environment. The central molecular zone of our Galaxy (CMZ; i.e. the inner few hundred parsecs) hosts some of the most extreme products of the SF process. These include massive star clusters (e.g. the Arches and Quintuplet), massive star-forming protocluster clouds (e.g. Sgr B2), and massive quiescent infrared dark clouds (e.g. G0.253+0.016, A.K.A “the Brick”). However, in spite of this, and despite containing ~80% per cent of the Galaxy’s dense gas, the global present-day SF rate is about 10-100x below what is expected given this vast reservoir of dense material.

Star formation within this complex environment may be closely linked to the orbital dynamics of the gas. Observational studies of some of the CMZ’s most massive and dense molecular clouds (a region of the CMZ known as the “dust ridge”), led to suggestions that their embedded star formation may follow an evolutionary sequence. In this interpretation, star formation is triggered following the compression of the gas clouds as they make their closest approach to the bottom of the Galactic potential well during their orbit. Verifying such a scenario necessitates obtaining an accurate, holistic understanding of the gas kinematics and distribution - something which has been sought after for several decades.

Figure 1: An {l, b, v} diagram highlighting the kinematic structure of the GC. Each data point refers to the location and velocity of an individual spectral feature extracted using SCOUSE. The kinematics are clearly complex. The complex velocity structure makes values derived from e.g. moment analysis highly unreliable. Accurate comparison between theory and observations therefore necessitates suitably detailed methodology.

Prior to 2016, the kinematics of the CMZ had been described using a combination of position-velocity diagrams, channel maps, and moment analysis. Unfortunately however, although such techniques are simple to implement and well-understood, their output can be subjective and easily misinterpreted in complex environments. With this in mind, we developed SCOUSE - a unique line-analysis tool designed to accurately extract the kinematics of star forming regions. I used SCOUSE to perform a comprehensive decomposition of the kinematics of the CMZ (see Fig. 1). Comparing with theoretical models for the orbital geometry this is now helping to constrain the 3D structure of the gas and helping us to understand the initial condtions for star formation in this complex environment. Moreover, In demonstrating that the dust ridge clouds belong to a single coherent gas stream, this work also confirmed the plausibility that these clouds follow an evolutionary sequence.

My study of the CMZ gas kinematics has also prompted new questions, for instance, is it reasonable to assume that the dust ridge clouds had similar initial conditions, such that the tidal compression at pericentre causes them to evolve and form stars on similar time-scales? To answer this question, we investigated the gas situated upstream from pericentre. I identified line-of-sight velocity corrugations as a function of Galactic longitude in this upstream gas. At the velocity extremes, the gas stream hosts compact (R ~ 2pc) cloud condensations with masses of ~10000 solar masses, that are quasi-regularly spaced with a separation of ~10 pc. The wavelength of the velocity corrugations and the separation of the clouds closely match the predicted Toomre length and Jeans length, respectivley. This led me to suggest that molecular clouds within the gas stream may form via gravitational instabilities. The similar spacing of the dust ridge clouds (~12 pc) suggests these clouds may formed in the same way. Situated upstream from pericentre, the newly identified condensations, with no signatures of star formation activity, may reflect historical analogues to the dust ridge clouds - and therefore progenitors of some of the most massive and dense molecular clouds in the Galaxy. If confirmed, these clouds may pinpoint the initial phases of star formation; the beginning of an evolutionary sequence that we can follow, as a function of time, from cloud condensation to star formation.
