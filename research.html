Star formation in the Galactic Centre

Molecular gas kinematics within the inner 250 pc of the Milky Way

The ultimate goal of star formation research is to develop an end-to-end understanding of stellar mass assembly as a function of environment. The central molecular zone of our Galaxy (CMZ; i.e. the inner few hundred parsecs) hosts some of the most extreme products of the SF process. These include massive star clusters (e.g. the Arches and Quintuplet), massive star-forming protocluster clouds (e.g. Sgr B2), and massive quiescent infrared dark clouds (e.g. G0.253+0.016, A.K.A “the Brick”). However, in spite of this, and despite containing ~80% per cent of the Galaxy’s dense gas, the global present-day SF rate is about 10-100x below what is expected given this vast reservoir of dense material.

Star formation within this complex environment may be closely linked to the orbital dynamics of the gas. Observational studies of some of the CMZ’s most massive and dense molecular clouds (a region of the CMZ known as the “dust ridge”), led to suggestions that their embedded star formation may follow an evolutionary sequence. In this interpretation, star formation is triggered following the compression of the gas clouds as they make their closest approach to the bottom of the Galactic potential well during their orbit. Verifying such a scenario necessitates obtaining an accurate, holistic understanding of the gas kinematics and distribution - something which has been sought after for several decades.
